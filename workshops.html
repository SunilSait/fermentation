Fermentation Timeline

What Happens During Fermentation

Understanding the stages of fermentation helps you create consistently delicious results.

Day 1–2: Initial Phase

Salt draws moisture from vegetables through osmosis. Lactic acid bacteria (LAB) begin to colonize. Brine forms naturally.

Day 3–5: Active Fermentation

Lactobacillus bacteria dominate. pH drops rapidly below 4.6. Bubbling increases as CO₂ is produced. Tangy flavors develop.

Day 6–14: Flavor Development

Slower fermentation deepens complexity. Beneficial bacteria multiply. Texture firms up. pH stabilizes around 3.5.

Day 14+: Maturation

Full flavor profile achieved. Move to cold storage to slow fermentation. Flavors continue to mellow and integrate over months.

Troubleshooting Guide

Common Fermentation Issues & Fixes

Don't worry — even experts encounter these. Here's how to diagnose and fix common problems.

Kahm yeast (white, powdery film) is harmless — just skim it off. Fuzzy mold (green, black, or pink) means contamination. Discard the batch. Prevention: Keep vegetables submerged below brine, use a fermentation weight, and maintain a clean environment.
Use 2% salt by weight of your vegetables. Too salty? Add a splash of non-chlorinated water. Not salty enough? Fermentation may be too fast — taste daily and refrigerate when tangy. Always weigh salt with a kitchen scale for precision.
Carbonation happens in the second fermentation (sealed bottle phase). Make sure: (1) use airtight flip-top bottles, (2) add a small amount of sugar or fruit juice, (3) leave at room temperature for 2–4 days, (4) burp bottles daily to prevent over-pressure.
Soft texture usually means: (1) fermentation went too long — taste daily and refrigerate earlier, (2) temperature was too high — ferment at 65–75°F (18–24°C), (3) not enough salt — increase to 2.5% for crunchier results. Adding a grape leaf or oak leaf provides natural tannins that keep vegetables crisp.
A sour, tangy smell is normal — that's lactic acid. A rotten, putrid smell means bad bacteria won. Causes: not enough salt, vegetables not submerged, contaminated tools. When in doubt, trust your nose. If it smells like it shouldn't be eaten, don't eat it.
